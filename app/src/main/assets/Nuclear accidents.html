Nuclear accidents

A nuclear and radiation accident is defined by the IAEA as “an event that has led to significant consequences to people, the environment of the facility.” Example includes lethal effects individuals, large radioactivity release to the environment or reactor core melt.


Causes of nuclear accidents

The reason may be one of several factors, including a loss of pressure control accident, a loss of coolant accident, an uncontrolled power excursion or in reactors without a pressure vessel, a fire within a reactor core. Failures in control systems may cause a series of events resulting in loss of cooling.


Diseases occur during nuclear accidents

Symptoms may include skin burns, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, hair loss, general weakness and possibly death. In addition to personal health concerns, there are also environmental health concerns associated with nuclear power generation. Nuclear power plants use water from local lakes and rivers for cooling.


Prevention

  • Stay away from any obvious plume or dust cloud. This will reduce exposure to any radioactive airborne dust.
  • Walk inside a building with closed doors and windows as quickly as possible and listen for information from emergency responders and authorities.
  • Turn on local radio or TV channels for advisories from emergency responses and health authorities.
  • Take a shower to wash off dust and dirt. This will reduce total radiation explosive, if the explosive device contained radioactive material.
  • If facilities are available, remove clothes and place them in a sealed plastic bag. Saving contaminated clothing will allow testing for radiation exposure.

Preparedness

  • Go indoors. Stay inside.
  • Close doors/windows.
  • Switch on the radio and look out for public announcements from local authority.
  • Cover all food, water and consume only such covered items.
  • If in the open, cover your face and body with a wet handkerchief, towel, dhoti or sari.
  • Extend full cooperation to local authorities and obey their instructions completely.

Recovery

  • From the outset, there has been a strong awareness of the potential hazard of both nuclear critically and release of radioactive materials from generating electricity with nuclear power.
  • As in other industries, the design and operation of nuclear power plants aims to minimize the likelihood of accidents and avoid major human consequences when they occur.
  • The evidence over six decades shows that nuclear power is a safe means of generating electricity. The risk of accidents in nuclear power plants is low and declining.
